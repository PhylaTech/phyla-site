First, learn to see.

July's proteins share one job: they make the invisible parts of a living cell show themselves.

Before you can study a thing, you have to see it, and for most of biology's history the most interesting things were far too small and too clear to watch at work. This month's four proteins each solved a piece of that problem.

One glows on its own. One makes light from a chemical reaction. One turns colorless colonies blue. One took the first and painted it in a range of new colors. Together they turned the microscope from a way of looking at dead, stained tissue into a way of watching life happen in real time.

We begin, fittingly, with the jellyfish protein that started it all.

In this collection
  1. Green Fluorescent ProteinA jellyfish's glow, turned into a universal tag.
  2. Firefly luciferaseLight on demand, powered by ATP.
  3. Beta-galactosidase (LacZ)The blue-white readout that made cloning legible.
  4. DsRed and mCherryThe palette that took the glow beyond green.
